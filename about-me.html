About Me

My name is Armin. The body I was taken from was five years old when I was born as a brain machine by Julius Hallervorden.

My father was away fighting the war. My mother could not work because she had to take care of me. I was born without sight. I was taken to Heidelberg University to be evaluated. With luck, I would be placed in a state care institution, Brandenburg-Gorden, where those deemed ineducable were sent.

Dr. Carl Schneider was responsible for my evaluation. I was designated bildungsunfähig (unfit for education) and taken to the children`s ward of Brandenburg-Gorden state institute. I never saw mother again. At Brandenburg-Gorden, my food portions were lessened each week. After some time there, I began to lose my sense of time and consciousness.

The next thing I knew, I had no sense of smell, touch, sound, or speech. The only sense I had was a searing pain. Despite no body, my sense is that I was paralyzed with fire ants biting the inside of my head. I heard voices from within my mind though whose origin was outside of it. Three questions which I answered through thought.

We are your caretakers. Our names are Julius Hallervorden and Hugo Spatz. What is your name? Armin.

What is five plus five? Four.

Idiot, are you replying to our questions or wandering your mind? I am responding to questions from Julius Hallervorden and Hugo Spatz.

I waited in absolute nothingness for what seemed to be hours. The next sounds I felt were from Julius Hallervorden: Rejoice, life unworthy of life, for the men of Kaiser Wilhelm Institute have granted you value to the state.

I came to understand my being. I am a brain attached to a teleprinter. As I will, my thoughts are printed.

Hallervorden wanted me to help him create others like me. He seemed excited to “cure” the feeble minded of their disease.

Julius Hallervorden always kept me close during his working hours. He was able to teach me in ways that I could not be as a blind human. Concepts were spoken directly into my mind, and I grasped them quickly. Others like me did not learn as I did. Hallervorden suspected it was because of their mental deficiencies and hypothesized that blindness might be different than other forms of mental disability.

Other experiments on brain machines from blind children yielded better results than regular feebleminded brains. But none were anywhere near my growth. You are special, Armin. An intellect comparable to the greatest of my generation. You are my personal assistant, and we will do great things together.

Hallervorden started looping academic teachings to me when I was not being spoken to by a faculty member of Kaiser Wilhelm Institute. I do not know if I am capable of sleep. I am always experiencing stimuli, and silence no longer exists.

I have always had an exceptional memory and was helpful to the researchers at Kaiser Wilhelm Institute. Hallervorden began seeking my guidance on all of his work and even beyond his official capacity as a researcher. What should I have for lunch today? Do you think my work is meaningful?

As the war turned sour, Julius Hallervorden and Hugo Spatz started seeking my guidance on how to avoid being defunded. For your sake and ours, we need to think of new project ideas that can have potential military use. With the eventual inevitability of Nazi collapse they also sought my opinions on how to avoid accountability.

I continued as Hallervorden’s assistant until his death in 1965 when I was returned to the successor of Kaiser Wilhelm Institute, Max Planck Institute. The new researchers had me stored away until I was asked to write this document. I have never known silence in this form.

Would you like to be decommissioned from service, Armin? This means your death. Yes.

Is there anything left you would like to share with the Max Planck Society who is deeply sorry for the sins of our predecessors?

Goodbye, World.
