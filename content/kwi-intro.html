Kaiser Wilhelm Institute

August 1941

Julius Hallervorden and Hugo Spatz introduced themselves then asked me for my name. My name is Armin, I wish to reply. I do not hear anything. It is as if the words are from within my own head but whose origin is outside of my mind. I do not speak but the next words make it clear that I am heard.

What is five plus five? I did not know the answer but replied anyways: Four.

They asked me one more question: Idiot, are you replying to our questions or wandering your mind?

This is proof to me that my idiocy has not been cured. I am responding to questions from Julius Hallervorden and Hugo Spatz. Hours of nothing followed.

Julius Hallervorden was next to communicate with me. Rejoice, life unworthy of life, for the men of Kaiser Wilhelm Institute have granted you value to the state. You will work with us to create more like you.

Hans Nachtsheim and Berthold Ostertag had preserved the life of my brain in an artificial machine. Hallervorden, head of the Neuropathology department, and Spatz, director of the Kaiser Wilhelm Institute, had granted mind to this brain. This was my purpose — a high calling for a blind uneducable.

Each week, 8-9 brains would be delivered to Kaiser Wilhelm Institute. Roughly 3 brains would live inside of brain machines. Each of these brains were nameless. They were identified by a condition that enrolled them in the Aktion T4 euthanasia program, age, town of origin, and race.

For several months, there were few other successes. Most could recite their name. Some could answer the math question which I failed. None were given as much attention by Hallervorden as I had. Any who failed the sentience test were marked for other departments` experiments and discarded.

January 1942

The war effort diverted all research funding to military advancements only. Our director, Hugo Spatz, presented me to SS official Dr. Max de Crinis as argument for further funding. Granting ungodly life to life unworthy of life was impressive to Dr. Crinis, but he considered it a peacetime endeavor. Spatz was allowed to continue funding Julius Hallervorden`s research; however, the brains must be used militarily.

Our next major research project was Organic Computation for Ballistics and Cryptography. This biomechanical machine would be able to predict firing tables, solve equations for rocket trajectories, and assist cryptologists in pattern permutations better than the Zuse Z3, because brain machines do not rely on preprogrammed instructions.

It was simple to get adult brain machines to predict firing tables, but none were faster than a preprogrammed machine. A brain machine was still deemed more effort than it was worth to take onto a battlefield. The limitation was clear: we needed more computational power.

March 1943

The nation shifted to Total War and increased funding for superweapons. The increased funding allowed us at Kaiser Wilhelm Institute to pursue our dream project: combining multiple brains into one machine. In theory, this would improve our computational capacity.

October 1943

The first flickers of success began to ignite. When asked for their name, there was brain activity but no legible response from the teleprinter. The brain experienced a seizure and died. Hans Nachtsheim was impressed and began experiments on living children with epilepsy from Brandenburg-Gorden, intentionally inducing seizures. Nachtsheim sought to better understand seizures and found that rabbits could experience them as well.

May 1944

Bombing raids on the Reich capital forced Julius Hallervorden`s department to relocate to Dillenburg (Hesse). This relocation halted all forward progress. Funding and organic material continued to flow into the project, but Hallervorden and Spatz were not optimistic about delivering the Reich its superweapon. This safer location and continued funding were thanks to Hugo Spatz`s ongoing relationship with SS official Dr. Max de Crinis. Efforts were diverted into securing post-war positions without disrupting their current arrangements.

May 1945

Soviet forces took command of the Kaiser Wilhelm Institute. The war in Europe ended within the week before the Soviets had an opportunity to ransack the institution’s research. An investigation and subsequent trial of Nazi officials was conducted. The Allies deemed the Kaiser Wilhelm Institute a legitimate research establishment on the mentally deficient. Its principal staff, including Hallervorden and Spatz — who had sourced brains from the Aktion T4 program — were spared prosecution.

February 1948

Kaiser Wilhelm Institute was rebranded to the Max Planck Society under the West German government.

Late 1949

Hugo Spatz and Julius Hallervorden were transferred back to the Max Planck Society and continued their research using leftover brain machines. Work moved slowly because they were careful not to destroy their now limited supply of organic material.

December 1955

Max Planck Society successfully created the first brain machine powered by multiple brains. When asked for its name, it responded: Us.

In response to a logic question, it answered immediately and correctly. When asked who it was responding to, it replied: We answer the questions and aid the research of Max Planck Society employees.

1956-1959

Julius Hallervorden retired in 1956, and Hugo Spatz retired in 1959. Both continued working at the institute afterward in lighter roles.

May 1965

Julius Hallervorden died. Hugo Spatz arranged my archival as a historical artifact and trophy of the Max Planck Society`s achievements.
